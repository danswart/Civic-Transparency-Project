Askable — The Internal-Relief Pitch

Civic Data Access · Model B (the jurisdiction keeps custody)

Author

Working strategy document

Published

May 16, 2026

How to read this document

This is not a marketing brochure. It is a thinking tool — written to be read and re-read until the reframe is automatic.

The single idea to internalize:

The institution is not buying transparency. It is buying relief from its own pain. The citizen benefit is a side effect we mention second — never the argument.

If at any point a draft, a slide, or a sentence starts selling civic virtue to the buyer, it has drifted back to the pitch that fails in the room. Come back here.

The wedge, in two paragraphs (the version that must hold up)

(This is the test. If these two paragraphs feel true without the words “transparency” or “citizen” doing the persuading, the wedge is real.)

You already have the data. Every budget figure, every staffing number, every reported total began life in an Excel file on your finance server — long before it became a PDF on the website. The problem isn’t that the information is missing. The problem is that your own people can’t get answers out of it either. A council member asks what changed in a budget line over five years and it takes a staffer half a day. A records request comes in and someone re-derives an answer the documents already contain. Institutional memory walks out the door with every departure.

Askable installs against the source files you already hold and makes them answerable in plain language — first for your staff. “What did we budget for this line in each of the last six years?” returns a sourced answer, with the file and figure it came from, in seconds instead of an afternoon. It runs on your data, on your terms, in your environment. We set it up and step back; custody never leaves your office. The fact that residents can then self-serve the routine questions — and stop calling your staff to ask them — is a benefit you get for free. It is not the reason to do this. The reason to do this is that your own team gets its time back.

Who actually feels the pain (the real buyer)

Not the transparency advocate. That person doesn’t exist inside the building, and selling to them is the pitch that fails. The buyer is whoever is exhausted by their own inaccessible records:

  • The clerk drowning in records requests that the documents could answer without human labor.
  • The new council member who cannot get a straight, fast answer about what was decided before they arrived.
  • The finance director who can’t quickly reconstruct a budget line’s history when the manager asks “why is this up?”
  • The line staffer who loses a day answering a question the record already contains.
  • The administrator watching institutional knowledge leave with every retirement and resignation.

None of these people care about the mission. All of them want their afternoon back. That want is the entire market.

Why this is Model B (and why that matters to the buyer)

Model B is not a deployment detail. It is the reason a defensive, information-averse institution can still say yes.

What it means Why the buyer cares
Their data, their custody Askable runs against the source files the jurisdiction already holds. Nothing is extracted, published, or sent out. No new exposure. No “are we releasing more than we must?” objection.
No FOIA, internally Inside the building it is their own file on their own drive. FOIA is irrelevant to the customer. The thing they’re defensive about (outside extraction) simply isn’t what’s happening.
Install and step back We stand it up against their environment; ongoing custody, storage, and control stay with them. No dependency on a vendor holding their data forever. No treadmill they’re signing up for.
Internal-first value The first visible result is their staff answering questions faster. The benefit lands on the person who signs, not on an abstract public.

The honest line for the room:

“You’re not exposing anything. You’re not sending us your data. You’re not doing extra work for the public. You’re pointing a tool at files you already have so your own people stop spending afternoons on questions the documents can already answer.”

The pitch, sequenced (say it in this order — order is the strategy)

  1. Name their pain, not our mission. “Your staff spends real time answering questions your own documents already contain. Records requests, council questions, budget history — re-derived by hand, over and over.”

  2. Locate the cause without blame. “It’s not a staffing problem. The data exists — it just isn’t answerable. Everything starts in Excel and ends in a PDF, and the PDF can’t be questioned.”

  3. Offer the relief, on their terms. “Askable installs against the source files you already hold and makes them answerable in plain English — for your team first. Your data stays with you. We set it up and step back.”

  4. Mention the public benefit second, as deflection, not virtue. “And because the routine questions become self-serve, residents stop calling your staff to ask them. Fewer repetitive requests is a side effect — a welcome one.”

  5. Stop. Do not argue democracy. The room already said yes for its own reasons. The mission was served through the back door.

Words to keep out of the buyer conversation

These belong in the investor deck and the public-facing story — not in the sentence that asks an administrator to sign:

  • “Transparency”
  • “Accountability”
  • “The public’s right to know”
  • “Hold officials accountable”
  • “Watchdog”

Each one reframes the tool as the citizen’s weapon — exactly the lens that makes a defensive institution balk. The relief pitch never needs them, and is stronger without them.

The honest limits (so the reframe doesn’t curdle into overconfidence)

  • The most defensive ~20–30% still won’t buy. A tool that makes records answerable internally also makes them answerable externally, and the bunker-mentality institutions will sense that and decline. That is fine. The market is the exhausted-but-pragmatic majority, not everyone.

  • Skewed evidence base. Every hostile interaction in memory came through FOIA — as an adversary, asking a known information-seeker’s question. That sample shows how institutions treat outside requesters. It says almost nothing about how a finance director responds to “this makes your own team faster.” Different conversation, never yet had.

  • Install-day normalization is the real hard problem. The first setup against a jurisdiction’s idiosyncratic decade of spreadsheets (2016 “Police” → 2023 “Public Safety”) cannot be fully automated. If every install requires artisan-grade reconciliation by hand, Model A’s treadmill has been quietly rebuilt on install day. The feasibility question worth funding: can cross-year normalization become a guided setup rather than a bespoke consulting engagement? Lived experience reconciling 29 years of TEA data is exactly what qualifies an honest answer here — keep it central, not buried under a clean demo.

The one-sentence company line

Civic Data Access builds Askable: it makes the records a jurisdiction already holds answerable in plain language — so the people who work there stop spending afternoons on questions the documents can already answer.

Transparency is what happens anyway. It is never what we sell.

Why this was hard to see (the note to self)

The FOIA-analyst vantage point — outsider, prying data loose, treated as a threat — quietly shaped the mental model of the whole company toward the adversarial frame and the maintenance treadmill. The fear was not wrong; it was reporting something true (don’t sell virtue to the virtue-averse). The error was the conclusion drawn from it (therefore no buyer). The correct, narrower conclusion: no buyer for the mission — a real buyer for the relief.

Re-read until that distinction is reflexive.
